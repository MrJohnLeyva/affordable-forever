There is a date on which my building stopped being affordable housing.

You would think that date would be written down somewhere. A regulatory agreement is a contract with the public — public money went in, and a term went with it. Thirty years. It ought to be as knowable as a mortgage maturity or a lease end.

It wasn't.

## What actually happened

When it became clear the Low-Income Housing Tax Credit on 63 Tiffany Place was running out, the obvious first question was: *when, exactly?*

Nobody could answer it.

Our attorneys had one date. The landlord's attorneys — and these are not amateurs, this is a major firm — had another. The landlord himself did not appear to be certain. Different documents pointed in different directions. Depending on what you counted from, and what you counted as the start, you could arrive at more than one answer.

So the date on which seventy families could lose their homes was not established. It was **agreed to**. We settled on one, because we had to settle on something, and the other side did not fight us hard on it.

Think about what that means. The single most consequential fact in our lives — the day the protection ends — was not a matter of record. It was a matter of negotiation.

## This is not a clerical problem

It would be easy to file this under bureaucratic mess. Paperwork gets lost. Buildings change hands. Ours has an unusual history — a failed condo conversion, a foreclosure, a sale to Related, a sale to the current owner in 2010.

But I don't think that's what this is. I think this is what happens when a program is designed with an exit and no landing.

Consider what the federal government *does* track carefully in LIHTC. The credits. The investor's basis. The ten-year claim period. The fifteen-year compliance window during which the IRS can recapture. All of that is documented, audited, and enforced, because money is at stake and the money belongs to someone with lawyers.

What isn't tracked with the same care is the thing that matters to the person living in the apartment: **when does this end, and what happens to me.**

That's not an oversight in the paperwork. It's an accurate reflection of what the program is actually about.

## The database has to guess

Here's how you know this is systemic and not just us.

The National Housing Preservation Database — maintained by the National Low Income Housing Coalition and PAHRC, the best public source that exists — estimates LIHTC expiration dates by taking the year a property was placed in service and **adding thirty**.

That's not a filed date. That's an assumption. It's the best they can do, because in many cases there is nothing better to go on.

A federal preservation database is estimating a date that ought to be a matter of public record. It will be too early where a state required a longer term. It will be too late where an owner slipped out early through the qualified contract loophole. And there's a whole category of properties the NHPD marks **inconclusive** — where the end date simply cannot be determined.

Read that again. There are buildings in this country where nobody knows when the affordability ends.

## What it does to you

I want to be plain about what living inside that uncertainty is like, because policy conversations tend to skip it.

You cannot plan a year ahead. You do not make large purchases. You keep an emergency fund you are afraid to spend, because you don't know what it's for yet — a move, a lawyer, a deposit somewhere you haven't chosen. Every piece of mail from the landlord is a small event. You find out, gradually, that you have organized your life around a date you cannot verify.

And you watch your neighbors get older inside it. People who moved in at fifty are in their eighties now. We've buried some of them. One of the founders of our tenants association traveled to Albany to lobby on our behalf and did not live to see how it ends.

## The fix is embarrassingly small

New York is on the verge of establishing that tenants have a **right to know**. Assembly bill A659 — which passed both houses in 2026 — requires a landlord to state, in writing, in at least twelve-point type, the exact date a 421-a tax benefit expires. Lying about it carries a fine.

It does not cover LIHTC-only buildings.

So under the law New York is about to have: a 421-a tenant is entitled to be told the date they may lose their home. A LIHTC tenant is not.

The principle is already conceded. The drafting simply doesn't reach us. Extending it is not a new idea, it's not expensive, and no landlord can argue against it without saying out loud that tenants shouldn't know when they'll be displaced.

That's the ask. It came from a kitchen table, from a question we could not get answered:

**When do we lose our homes?**

Someone should have to tell us.
