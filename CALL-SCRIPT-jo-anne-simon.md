# Calling Assemblymember Jo Anne Simon's office

**About:** A659 — the bill requiring landlords to disclose, in writing, the exact date a tax benefit expires. It passed both houses in 2026. **It does not cover LIHTC-only buildings.**

**Your ask:** extend it to LIHTC.

---

## Before you dial — the three things you need

**1. Where the bill actually is right now.** It passed the Assembly May 5 and the Senate May 28, 2026. It may be with the Governor, it may already be signed, it may not have been delivered yet. **You don't need to know — that's the first thing you're calling to find out.** Do not pretend you know.

**2. Your standing, in one sentence.** *"I'm a constituent, I live at 63 Tiffany Place, and my LIHTC agreement expired."*

**3. The ask, in one sentence.** *"A659 gives 421-a tenants the right to know their expiration date. It doesn't cover LIHTC-only buildings like mine. I want it extended."*

That's it. Everything else is conversation.

---

## The script

**When they pick up:**

> Hi, my name is ____. I'm a constituent — I live at 63 Tiffany Place, in the Columbia Street Waterfront District.
>
> I'm calling about **A659**, the Assemblymember's bill on disclosure of tax benefit expiration dates. I have two things: a question, and an ask.

*(Pause. Let them get a pen.)*

**The question:**

> First — can you tell me where the bill stands? My understanding is it passed both houses back in May. Has it been delivered to the Governor? Has she signed it?

*(Write down exactly what they say. This is intelligence you don't have.)*

**The ask:**

> Second, and this is the reason I'm calling.
>
> A659 requires a landlord to tell a tenant, in writing and in twelve-point type, the exact date their tax benefit expires. That's a good bill and I want it signed.
>
> **But it only reaches 421-a buildings.** My building is LIHTC — the low-income housing tax credit — with no 421-a on it. So under A659, a 421-a tenant is entitled to know the date they may lose their home, and I am not.
>
> And I need you to understand why that matters to me personally. **When our agreement was expiring, nobody could tell us the date.** Our lawyers had one date. The landlord's lawyers had another. The landlord himself wasn't sure. In the end we *negotiated* it — the day seventy families could lose their homes was settled by agreement, because nobody could establish it from a record.
>
> So my ask is: **extend the same disclosure requirement to LIHTC.** The Assemblymember has already voted for this principle. The bill language already exists. This is an amendment, not a new idea.

**Then stop talking.** Let them respond.

---

## Likely responses, and what to say

**"I'll pass it along to the Assemblymember."**
> Thank you. Can I send you the specifics in writing so you have the bill number and the language? What's the best email?

*(This is the goal of the call. Get the email address. Then send the letter.)*

**"Have you been in touch with Assemblymember Rosenthal's office?"** *(She's the lead sponsor.)*
> Not yet — I'm starting with my own member. Would you be willing to raise it with her office? I'd rather this come from a colleague than a stranger.

**"Session is over, nothing can happen until January."**
> I understand. That's exactly why I'm calling now — so it can be drafted over the interim and introduced early, instead of us starting from zero in the spring. And if the Governor hasn't signed A659 yet, that's a separate and more urgent thing.

**"Send us an email."**
> I will, today. Can I ask one thing first — would the Assemblymember be willing to meet? She's written letters on behalf of my building before, and I think seeing what happened to us would be useful.

---

## Two things to remember

**Do not be apologetic.** Jo Anne Simon has already signed letters on behalf of 63 Tiffany Place. She has stood outside your building. You are not a stranger asking for a favour — you are a constituent she has already gone to bat for, coming back with something specific and easy.

**The date story is your closer.** If the call drifts, come back to it. *"Two law firms couldn't tell me the date I'd lose my home."* Nobody in that office has heard that before, and nobody forgets it.
